Childcare Philosophy

  • Post by Bethany
  • Jan 01, 2020

My childcare philosophy hinges on the idea that children learn through interacting with their environment. This opens up into a whole web of topics, each intertwined with the rest. Maintaining a positive environment is important so the child feels safe to explore, try new things, and make mistakes without repercussions aside from the natural consequence. To one child, this might mean climbing just a little bit higher on the jungle gym. I’ll be there to catch them, but it is up to the child to test their limits in balance and strength, and learn what happens when they exceed their abilities. To another child, this could look like them trying out what happens if they start shouting and making demands, rather than asking for help. I’ll be their example and practice partner for how they can best ask questions and negotiate for things they want. As I keep an eye on their development, I will see what accomplishment they are nearing, and set up the environment to make that more accessible.

There is no one specific childcare philosophy that I adhere to, but I am constantly looking for new material that supports my basic idea about interacting with the environment. While I am not trained in RIE, I can use the idea of a “yes space” to better design an area for a baby to explore without needing constant input from me - and eliminating the need for me to disallow certain actions. While I wouldn’t necessarily call play “work” like Montessori teachers do, I love to involve children in everyday tasks like preparing meals, wiping up spills, and putting dirty clothes in the appropriate place. As a self-described “artsy-crafty” person, I’m drawn to the idea of Waldorf education and bring movement and visual arts into many academic or cognitive based goals, but I still see a role for store-purchased toys.

What seems to be the magic question these days: what are my views on screen time? If it is left 100% up to me, I only use screen time in very rare and special occasions. Sometimes, a surprise popcorn and movie night for school age kids can be timed perfectly to give them a relaxing night after a jam-packed week of school, homework, sports, and other activities. I tend to avoid it altogether for children preschool age and younger, though, unless it is already established in the family’s routine and the parents are happy the way things are. Otherwise, there’s just too many things to explore!

Helping to raise respectful, curious, and independent children is why I do what I do. I think Ms. Frizzle said it best: “Time to take chances, make mistakes, get messy!”
